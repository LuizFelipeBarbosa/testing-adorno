# The Algorithm as Culture Industry: Adorno's Critique in the Age of the "For You" Page

## (APA Citation Style)

---

Theodor Adorno died in 1969, decades before the first personal computer was manufactured or even the first recommendation algorithm was created. Yet, if one reads his critique of the "Culture Industry" today, it reads less like mid-century philosophy and more like a user manual for the TikTok "For You" page. On modern social media platforms, recommendation engines process raw user data---such as likes, views, and watch time---to build individual viewer profiles, allowing companies to predict user preferences, suggest relevant content, and sell products. However, through the lens of Theodor Adorno, this customized data is not proof of our individuality; rather, it enforces the "standardization" of our identity. It is the ultimate manifestation of "pseudo-individuality"---a system where the algorithm functions as an engine of "The Culture Industry," commodifying our media habits and selling our own conformity back to us as personal choice while we "passively" consume mass-produced slop.

This essay explores the premise that these recommendation engines have become the invisible curators of modern culture, fostering an uncritical consumption of media content. To do so, we will examine the extent to which Adorno's critiques of popular music are supported by real-world data. Through a quantitative analysis of user comments across the 15 most-streamed songs released in 2025, this paper demonstrates how digital engagement has largely standardized the listener's response rather than liberating it---while simultaneously enabling forms of discussion that Adorno's theory deemed impossible. Ultimately, the repetitive, identity-signaling nature of these comments over genuine musical critique confirms his theory of "regressive listening," revealing an audience that has become an echo chamber for the Culture Industry's own marketing.

---

## I. Standardization: The Algorithm's Template

In "On Popular Music," Adorno argues that the structure of popular music is "pre-given and pre-accepted, before the actual experience of the music starts" (Adorno, 1941, p. 17). Every element---verse, chorus, bridge---slots into a rigid template, making individual songs interchangeable units within an undifferentiated mass of product. The culture industry does not produce art; it produces variations on a formula.

The 15 most-streamed songs released in 2025 bear this out with striking uniformity. An NLP analysis of 84,816 YouTube comments across all 15 songs identified 81 comments classified under the "Standardization" critique label, with an average confidence score of 0.64 (Critique-Label Matched Comments, 2026). What is remarkable is not merely that the word "generic" appears---it is that it appears *across* artists and genres, suggesting listeners perceive a systemic condition rather than isolated cases. On Alex Warren's "Ordinary," @surferboypizza12 delivers a terse diagnosis: "this is literally a basic radio song, is so generic" (surferboypizza12, 2026). On Taylor Swift's "The Fate of Ophelia," @dominichern9446 broadens the indictment: "a generic boring comment for a generic boring song from a generic boring artist" (dominichern9446, 2025). On Tate McRae's "Sports car," @azraksash identifies the industrial machinery beneath the surface: "It actually sounds like generic K pop from like 5 years ago" (azraksash, 2025). Even on HUNTR/X's animated blockbuster "Golden," @The_Null_Theory names the system outright: "purposely generic mind rot songs for children to enjoy..not because they are actually good music" (The_Null_Theory, 2026).

But Adorno's concept gains new force when applied to the delivery mechanism itself. In "A Social Critique of Radio Music," Adorno warns that radio atomizes the musical experience, severing listeners from "the structural wholeness of the work" and reducing it to isolated fragments (Adorno, 1945, p. 211). Today's recommendation algorithms---Spotify's Discover Weekly, YouTube's autoplay, TikTok's "For You" page---do not merely distribute standardized music; they standardize the *context* of listening. A song is not encountered as a complete artistic statement but as a fifteen-second TikTok snippet, a playlist transition, a background loop. @iam1hobbit captures this perfectly regarding "Golden": "too many kpop songs today are custom built for tiktok - with choruses that are simply chanted with some viral dance move but without singing" (iam1hobbit, 2025). When the algorithm selects the next song for you, it is not curating your taste---it is, in Adorno's terms, pre-giving the whole before the experience begins.

## II. Pseudo-Individualization: Personalization as Conformity

Adorno defines pseudo-individualization as "endowing cultural mass production with the halo of free choice or open market on the basis of standardization itself" (Adorno, 1941, p. 25). The culture industry permits surface-level variation---a distinctive vocal timbre, a quirky aesthetic---precisely to disguise the underlying sameness of its products, granting the listener an illusion of meaningful personal taste.

The recommendation algorithm is pseudo-individualization at industrial scale. Spotify's "Made For You" playlists promise a listening experience tailored to your unique preferences. In practice, this "tailoring" draws from the same narrow pool of algorithmically favored tracks---the very 15 songs in this analysis, which appear on millions of personalized playlists simultaneously. Each listener believes they have discovered something personal; in reality, the algorithm has delivered the same commodity to everyone, dressed in the language of individual choice.

Commenters identify this mechanism with precision, even without the theoretical vocabulary. On Tate McRae's "Sports car," @bailee7696 offers a terse verdict: "literally are carbon copy of buttons" (bailee7696, 2025)---the only comment the NLP pipeline classified under the "Pseudo-Individualization" label at a confidence of 0.75, a phrase that could serve as a dictionary definition of the concept: surface novelty concealing structural sameness. On JENNIE's "like JENNIE," @petrovic3917 is blunter: "Ofc, who would want to be fake like her" (petrovic3917, 2026). On "Golden," @ruskiwaffle1991 names the system explicitly: "Funny thing is, K-pop is one of the most corporate music genres out there" (ruskiwaffle1991, 2025). @ZhonWeak extends the critique further, noting that "what makes a song nice isn't just the names on the song title, there are people not named in the title who are the very reason why the song is amazing, ie the songwriters (not the singers most of the time for commercial music) and the producers" (ZhonWeak, 2025). This comment identifies both halves of pseudo-individualization: the artist presents a false image of creative authorship, and the listener consumes a false image of individual artistry.

The broader data reinforces this pattern. The NLP pipeline flagged 14 comments under "Commodification / Market Logic" (average confidence 0.77), many of which centered on accusations of manufactured success and commercial calculation (Critique-Label Matched Comments, 2026). On "Golden," @fonumanukeu-bv9ty identifies the profit motive beneath the aesthetic surface: "it was probably just a money grab" (fonumanukeu-bv9ty, 2025). On Taylor Swift's "The Fate of Ophelia," @Aceley27 connects commodification to critical reception: "another piece of trash cash grab?!? How pathetic you have to pay to have enough nice stuff said about you" (Aceley27, 2026). We hear commenters articulating, in raw form, the core of Adorno's argument: that the industry manufactures both the product *and* the demand for it.

## III. Regressive Listening: The Echo Chamber as Audience

Adorno's most provocative claim is not merely that popular music is standardized but that it produces standardized *listeners*. In "On Popular Music," he describes a mode of reception in which the listener's response is "pre-determined by the mechanism of popular music itself" (Adorno, 1941, p. 26). Recognition replaces cognition; comfort replaces challenge. The listener does not evaluate---the listener *recognizes*, and this recognition is experienced as pleasure.

The comment sections beneath these 15 songs offer powerful evidence for this thesis. Of 84,816 comments analyzed, only 269---a mere 0.32%---contained any identifiable engagement with the mechanisms of cultural production (Critique-Label Matched Comments, 2026). The overwhelming majority consist of precisely the kind of pre-programmed, identity-signaling responses Adorno predicted: "This slaps," "Who's here in 2026?", "I'm not crying, you're crying." These are not evaluations of music; they are performances of consumption---ritualistic declarations of belonging that mirror the standardized product they accompany.

The comment section, mediated by the same algorithmic logic as the music itself, becomes an echo chamber. YouTube's sorting algorithm promotes comments with high engagement---typically the most generic, broadly relatable reactions---burying substantive critique beneath a wall of emojis and time-stamped affirmations. On Alex Warren's "Ordinary," @alwaysright3718 reduces the song to background noise: "its terrible, elevator music" (alwaysright3718, 2026). @Dr_Darrow is more explicit about the same track: "another ordinary overprocessed pile of garbage got a bunch of listens from the mass of braindead ordinaries" (Dr_Darrow, 2026). On HUNTR/X's "Golden," @The_Null_Theory names the audience condition directly: "purposely generic mind rot songs for children to enjoy..not because they are actually good music" (The_Null_Theory, 2026). This is the language of regressive listening rendered in the vernacular---the accusation that audiences have been conditioned into passivity by the very infrastructure they mistake for liberation.

## IV. Point of Disagreement: The Critical Listener Adorno Denied

The strongest counter-argument to Adorno does not dispute that standardization, pseudo-individualization, and regressive listening exist. It challenges his conclusion about their *totality*. For Adorno, the culture industry's mechanisms are so pervasive that genuine critical consciousness becomes virtually impossible for the mass listener. Yet the very data that confirms his critique also refutes this claim.

The 269 critique-bearing comments---the 0.32%---represent something Adorno's framework cannot accommodate: ordinary listeners who independently articulate precisely the kinds of culture-industry analysis that he reserved for the theorist's privileged vantage point. Consider @ivobaki3751's remarkable comment on HUNTR/X's "Golden":

> I know how things are behind curtains in kpop world and as they sing lovely like angels everything i know makes me more resistant to its influence. Kids went bzrk about them and im sure these ladies good persons and all but the industry that generated entire show is what it is. Ive seen same pattern repeat too many times. (ivobaki3751, 2026)

This commenter, without any evident exposure to Frankfurt School theory, identifies standardization ("same pattern repeat too many times"), pseudo-individualization (artists who "sing lovely like angels" while an industrial apparatus operates behind the curtain), and a conscious critical resistance to the product's affective pull in a single observation. Or consider @sandyg4646 on the same track: "k pop is extremely formulaic in its approach despite the mixing of different styles. You said it yourself 'more focus on choreography and stage presence' what does that tell you?" (sandyg4646, 2025). This is Adorno's argument about standardization extended to the genre's visual-industrial apparatus---a refinement the theorist himself might have appreciated. Even @estuchedepeluche2212, commenting on sombr's "undressed," reaches for literary theory to name the phenomenon: "Derivative, not quite original, familiar, comforting, but oh, so good. As Umberto Ecco said: 'The cliches are talking and they are having a ball!'" (estuchedepeluche2212, 2025).

If the culture industry truly produces the total passivity Adorno claims, these comments should not exist. Their existence reveals that mass audiences are not the undifferentiated, regressed consumers he imagined but a heterogeneous population containing individuals capable of recognizing and articulating the mechanisms of cultural production---even while consuming its products. The very platforms that deliver "Sports car" as a fifteen-second TikTok loop also host the critical discourse that dismantles its logic. Writing in the 1940s, Adorno could not have anticipated a media environment in which the consumer is simultaneously a commentator, a critic, and a broadcaster---nor one in which the tools of mass distribution would also function as platforms for the critical exchange he believed only serious art could provoke. The algorithm may be the new culture industry, but it has not yet managed to silence the 0.32%.
